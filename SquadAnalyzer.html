1.          Welcome to SQuad Analyzer

This document describes the SwissQual SQuad Analyzer product for the offline assessment of speech quality with the SQuad and ITU-T P.863/POLQA models. SQuad Analyzer is a Windows 32 and 64 bit program, which consists of a set of components to predict speech quality and the decomposition of degradation.

2.          Definition of Speech Quality

For network operators or equipment manufacturers, it is important to know where and why speech quality degrades. Since speech quality is a major factor in determining customer satisfaction, encoding techniques must be designed for optimal speech quality. In order to assess the quality of speech encoding techniques, large-scale auditory tests are commonly employed. However, it is practically impossible to reproduce the results that have been obtained in such tests. Such results also depend on the level of motivation of the individual test candidates. A better approach is to rely on instruments that are capable of physically measuring speech quality parameters and producing results, which correlate as closely as possible with subjectively acquired results. The speech encoders for digital mobile handsets in radio networks further impair intelligibility. Speech quality is a vague term with respect to bit rate, echo and loudness. Since customer satisfaction can be directly measured by the quality of the transmitted speech, encoding techniques must be selected and optimized based on speech quality.

Speech quality is a measure of a listeners satisfaction based on his or her experience and expectation of voice communication. Speech quality is generally expressed as a Mean Opinion Score (MOS), which is the average of many individual opinions on speech quality that have been obtained from a statistically representative number of listeners. Speech quality is a complex psycho-acoustic phenomenon within the process of human perception and as such is subjective. Most objective speech quality algorithms are based on a comparison between a reference sample and an encoded version of the reference.

3.          Recommendations for Source Speech Material

SwissQual can only guarantee proper operation of SQuad Analyzer with the speech content that is described in the following section.

A reference speech sample is the original speech signal with no degradation, which has been recorded and stored according to the information in Appendix II of P.863. For best results, use human speech for the content with typical spoken sentences and typical syllable and word structures. Single word reference samples only, for example, counting, are not recommended.

         Adjust the level of the reference signals to -26 dBov

         For reference samples with two male and two female speakers, use two sentences for each person

         In super-wideband operational mode, ITU-T P.863 requires a super-wideband reference signal. This signal must be in mono and have a sampling frequency that allows the representation of the full spectral range of super-wideband (50 to 14000 Hz), that is, 48 kHz. The signal must be pre-filtered by a 50 to 14000 Hz. For narrowband operational mode, a sampling frequency of 48 kHz can also be used.

         Each reference speech file must consist of two or more sentences that are separated by a gap of at least 1 s but not more than 2 s.

         Minimum amount of active speech in each file must be 3 s.

         To avoid clippings of the speech signal, reference speech files require sufficient leading and trailing silence intervals, for example, 200 ms

         For SWB reference speech samples the noise floor of the reference files must not exceed -84 dBov(A) in the leading and trailing parts as well as in the gaps between the sentences

         The room in which the reference samples are recorded must have a reverberation time below 300 ms above 200 Hz, for example, an anechoic chamber

         Recordings must be made with omnidirectional microphones. The distance to the microphone must be approximately 10 cm. Background noise must be below 30 dBSPL(A).

         Speech signals must be band-pass filtered to 20 Hz -14 kHz. Directional microphones are allowed on the condition that the frequency response is the same as the omni-directional microphones.

For more details on the speech material recommendations, see ITU-T P.863 (1/2011).

5.          Results

Average Parameters

The average parameter results are generated by Squad Analyzer command line tool and the GUI. An API interface allows also access to these parameters. Each voice probe has one set of parameters.

The MOS is a measure of a listeners speech quality satisfaction. Both SQuad and P.863 deliver MOS ratings as a single number from 1 to 5 in accordance to the Listening Scale in ITUs P.800 recommendation.

Status Code

0 = OK, MOS and other results are available.

Values greater than or less than 0 MOS and other results are meaningless. For example, a -5 value indicates that only Silence was detected whereas a -30 value refers to a Speech cut at the beginning of the probe, that is, the audio recording was started too late, or a cut off at end, that is, the audio recording was stopped too early.

Active Speech Level

Active speech level is defined by ITU-T rec. P.56 and is the r.m.s. level of the active speech portions only. Longer speech pauses or silence intervals do not influence this level. The r.m.s. level is measured in dB relative to the overload point (dB OVL), which is +32767 to -32768 in the case of 16bit representation.

Noise Level

Noise level describes the noise floor of the signals in speech pauses in dBov. In a normal noise-free connection a noise level below -55dBov can be obtained.

Missed Voice

The missed voice parameter is calculated as the percentage of muted or missing speech portions from the original speech content in the reference sample.

Delay Spread

The first stage in the algorithm is a time alignment step, which compares the received signal with the reference sample to detect fixed and variable delays between the two signals. The delay spread is the difference between the shortest and the largest delay in the signal. In a fixed (constant) delay, the delay spread is equal to 0 ms. Variable delays are especially frequent in packet switched networks.

DelaySpread=MaxDelay / SamplingFreq

Example: fix_delay=1024 samples, SamplingFreq=16000Hz,

DelaySpread=1024/16000=64ms

Delay Deviation

The delay deviation is the standard deviation of the delay changes that are calculated for short term frames. For fixed (constant) delay, the delay deviation is equal to 0 ms.

Offset at Signal Start (Start Delay)

Delay between the reference and the probe at the first occurrence of active speech.

Frequency Band Limits

Low and high bandwidth limits of active speech in the degraded probe signal expressed in Hz. More information about the frequency boundaries can be seen when the frequency response (Figure 5‑7) is presented graphically.

Degradation Code

Degradation codes are useful values where poor speech quality occurs. These values can help to determine the reasons for quality drops in order to improve the network devices that are responsible for voice quality.

The command line Squad Analyzer only generates Degradation code, whereas the GUI translates the codes into code text, which appears as the Degradation Analysis column. When MOS values dip below 3.8, degradation codes are set to true. To play back the voice file, double-click the results line of the corresponding file. The WAV and PCM files are played in the Windows default audio player for these file types.

Similarity

Similarity highly corelates with the speech quality within one frame (20ms). Such information is useful for investigating the source of quality issues as well as the temporal location of the issue in the probe. The value 1 represents ideal speech quality, that is, no difference between reference and probe, and 0 represents the worst speech quality.

The example in Figure 5‑2 shows two major drops in similarity at 1.25 s and at 2.4 s, which were probably caused by signal interruptions at these locations.

Figure 5‑2 Similarity vs. time

Signal Envelopes

Signal envelopes correspond to r.m.s. values of the time signal that is calculated for each speech frame. The speech frame length is 20ms. When presented together, the reference and degraded envelopes show the correctness of the time alignment algorithm, that is, when the time alignment is correct, the two curves overlap. Gain discrepancies can also be resolved with these two curves. The maximum r.m.s. values should never reach 0 dB unless there was amplitude saturation in the signal.

Figure 5‑3 Signal envelopes vs time

Perceptual Speech Signal-Loss (Interruptions)

Signal loss is only measured during perceptual speech active phases in the degraded signal. A loss of speech at one of these positions is perceived as a degradation. Figure 5‑4 shows an example with nine occurences of speech loss that result in poor MOS value.

The interruptions are mostly caused by the total loss of information, for example, in VoIP connections where the speech frames are dropped. The interruptions can also occur when voice activity detection (VAD) or Digital Circuit Multiplication Equipment (DCME) is used as well as during uncontrolled slips.

In SQuad Analyzer the time clipping is only detected during transitions from pause to speech when a minimum pause length of 64 milliseconds has been reached. The difference of Energy (reference) Energy (degraded) must be at least 10 dB. Clipped frames are succeeding frames.

Figure 5‑4 Speech signal loss vs. time

Signal Gain Trend

Signal amplification is an average value that is calculated over a sliding time window and is useful for spotting Active Gain Control (AGC) effects in the signal. Figure 5‑5 shows an approximate 2dB increased gain in the second half of the voice clip.

The value only reflects gain that is caused by the network. The signal gain trend is close to the so-called Junction Loudness Rating (JLR) but does not apply spectral weighting. In a transparent ISDN connection the Channel Gain is around 0 dB. In principle, this value should also be around 0dB in a Mobile-to-ISDN or Mobile-to-Mobile connection. However, this value might differ if individual signal amplifications of cellular network providers occur. If an overall gain of 6dB is exceeded, amplitude clipping can occur, which can lead to quality degradation and lower MOS scores.

The expected range of gain is +6 to -9 dB.

Figure 5‑5 Signal gain vs. time

Noise Level Trend of Probe

The noise level trend is an average value that is calculated over the time and is useful for spotting Noise Reduction (NR) effects in the signal. Figure 5‑6 shows a sudden noise increase of 1.5 dB in the second half of the clip.

Figure 5‑6 Noise level of probe vs. time

Frequency Response

Average frequency response of active speech is useful for checking signal bandwidth and signal coloration. This parameter is especially important when you compare narrow band and super wide-band signals.

7.          Appendix B Command Line Tool

SQuadAnalyzer AM2_fm_flat.wav AM2_fm_IRS_B.wav 1 0 >> results.txt

8.          Appendix C GUI

The SQuad Analyzer GUI is simple and self-explanatory. This application can only evaluate one pair of voice files at a time.

  • To add the files, drag the files onto the window or press the Open icon .
  • To select the speech quality evaluation method, that is, POLQA or SQuad, click in the Application list.
  • To select the evaluation mode, that is, SuperWideband or Narrowband, click in the Mode list.

Figure 8‑1 SQuad Analyzer GUI

  • To generate a results file, select the To File checkbox in the Results area.

This file is automatically stored in the voice clips folder after the evaluation of the voice files. The results file name uses the following syntax:

probeFileName.Application-Mode_DD.MM.YYYY_hh.mm.ss.ttt.csv

For example, for the following settings:

  • Probe name: AM2_fm_IRS_10percentFrameLoss.wav
  • Application: POLQA
  • Mode: SuperWideband
  • Date: 26th Oct 2011

The results file is:

AM2_fm_IRS_10percentFrameLoss.POLQA-SuperWideband_26.10.2011_13.52.23.689.csv

  • To export the average results to a file in CSV format (one line per file pair), click Save To on the Results menu.
  • To start the evaluation process, click Run on the Test menu, or press CTRL+R.

  • To clear the results table, click Clear Table on the Results menu.

Default location for config file in Windows 7 is: user\AppData\Roaming\AlgoTester
